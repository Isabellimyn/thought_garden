*Written for The Ridge NUSSU Magazine.*

Theoretically, “boys will be boys” and “I’m just a girl” should be direct counterparts of avoiding responsibility, but somehow, the addition of the word ‘just’ diminishes women in a way that we’re become scarily desensitised to.


If you think about it (and I have, perhaps too much), positioning yourself as "just" a girl while boys are a standalone category indirectly posits girls as helpless to be anything more. A boy gets to shed responsibility simply by existing as a biological fact; a girl has to actively plead the "girl" card to get out of a parallel park. It’s the difference between an excuse and a surrender.

Or maybe more and more seemingly harmless jokes that start as tongue-in-cheek feminism have accidentally circled back to demeaning femininity. Even trends that have nothing to do with gender—like sourdough and the pursuit of a rustic life—end up re-establishing a heavy dose of gender roles with trickles of 1950s-flavored oppression seeping through.

Before I start to sound like a loon, let me begin with patient zero.

### Covid and the stay-at-home everyone

Aside from the collective fever dream of the Renegade dance, TikTok’s trad-life scene was the biggest winner of 2020. Going out was a literal crime, so we all decided to roleplay as 19th-century peasants.

The rise of Ballerina Farm (Hannah Neeleman) provided a weird sort of hope in a tumultuous time. Baking from scratch and living a calm, minimalist, rural fantasy felt like the ultimate antidote to the "Girlboss" corporate hustle that was currently dying in a Zoom meeting. Hannah has nine kids, an apron that never gets stained, and she doesn’t technically claim the "trad-wife" label—none of them do. They’re too busy milking cows for that. By staying "apolitical," she’s protected her appeal to the masses, making the lifestyle look like a choice rather than a manifesto.

Then came the 2023 reinforcements: Nara Smith making bubblegum from scratch in a silk gown. These women aren't screaming "down with the 19th amendment," but in the digital age, your aesthetic is your politics. And the aesthetic currently says: I don't have a job, I have a whisk.

### Aprons, Eggs, and the Death of a Dream

The argument of this genre of content being apolitical falls apart when we mention the egg apron.

It sounds like a niche accessory for a hobbyist, but in the context of the Ballerina Farm empire, it’s a symbol. In a widely discussed profile of Hannah Neeleman, a specific detail stood out: for her birthday, she hoped for a trip to Greece. Instead, her husband bought her an apron with pockets for collecting eggs.

It was a "small thing." A domestic gift for a domestic woman. But that apron represents the crushing weight of a "choice" that isn't really a choice at all. Here is a woman—a Juilliard-trained ballerina, mind you—whose entire identity has been curated, packaged, and sold as a return to "simplicity," yet she can’t even escape the gravity of her husband’s vision for her life. 

It shows that misogyny is so deeply rooted that even an "apolitical" influencer with millions of dollars can be reduced to a vessel for labor and procreation. If the woman with the world at her fingertips is still "just a wife" getting an egg apron for her birthday, what hope is there for the rest of us?

This is where the "choice" argument falls apart. When these influencers present their lives as a series of aesthetic choices—baking bread, homeschooling, wearing modest dresses—they omit the structural reality that their lifestyle is built on the erasure of their own independent aspirations. The "choice" to be a trad-wife is only a choice if the alternative (having a career, traveling, not having nine children) is actually on the table. When the dream of Greece is replaced by an egg apron, the "choice" looks a lot more like a life sentence.

### Girl as a Linguistic Modifier

While the homesteaders were busy romanticizing the domestic sphere, the rest of the internet was busy gutting the English language. This is where "girl" became a linguistic modifier for literal brain-rot.

The prefix "girl" has a weird history. In the 90s, "Girl Power" was a threat—a demand for space. In 2014, "Girlboss" was a corporate strategy to make capitalism feminist. But once Megan Thee Stallion’s "Hot Girl Summer" made it okay to put "girl" in front of everything, we stopped using it for empowerment and started using it for evasion.

A Girlboss would NOT be succumbing to girl math nor girl dinner - so what happened?

Somehow, “girl” became a linguistic modifier for trivialization, irony, or identity framing. We stopped being women and started ‘girlling’.

Girl Dinner: What started as a relatable joke about eating a plate of olives and a string cheese for dinner quickly devolved into a weird glorification of disordered eating disguised as "low effort" whimsy.

Girl Math: If I pay with cash, it’s free. If I return something for $50 and spend $100, I actually made $50. It’s funny, sure, but it also leans heavily into the "women are bad at money" trope that kept us from having bank accounts until the 70s. 

Girlboss: The precursor to it all. It tried to make capitalism cute, but just ended up making burnout pink. Now that "Girlbossing" is dead, we’ve swung the pendulum so far back that we’re “waiting in our boyfriends’ cars while they’re at their big-boy job”.

When we use "Girl" to describe basic human functions (Girl Hobby, Girl Walk, Girl Job), we aren't reclaiming femininity—we’re infantalizing it. We’ve turned "Woman" into a scary, tax-paying monster and "Girl" into a safe, dainty category where you don’t have to understand how interest rates work.

### Homesteading and girl link

This is where the sourdough meets the "just a girl" audio. Homesteading gave the "girl" prefix a physical location—the kitchen—where "Girl Math" and "Girl Dinner" could live. The homesteading movement is, at its core, a fantasy of childhood. It’s about being taken care of, living in a protected bubble, and focusing on the tactile, simple world of the nursery.

By labeling our lives with "girl," we are digitally homesteading. We are retreating into a version of femininity that is purposefully small, manageable, and unthreatening. The "Girl" prefix is the linguistic version of the Egg Apron. It’s a gift we give ourselves to feel "cute" while we simultaneously surrender our status as competent, functioning adults.

### Double standards

Sadly, that’s just the environment today. When boys joke about being a "stay-at-home boyfriend" or getting "beat up by their girl," it’s funny because it’s subversive. He’s joking about losing power he actually possesses in the real world.

But when girls lean into the "I’m just a girl" bit or the "I wish I was a 1950s housewife" aesthetic, it’s not subversive. It’s reinforcing centuries of misogyny that we’re still desperately trying to lacerate. We are cosplaying as the people our grandmothers fought and bled so that we wouldn't have to be.

### The Takeaway

The takeaway here isn’t that you should throw away your sourdough starter or stop wearing pink. It’s that we need to be incredibly wary of any trend that asks us to play "small" for a laugh. Whether it's an "apolitical" influencer showing you her nine kids or a TikTok filter that makes you feel cute for not knowing how to change a tire, the goal is the same: to make the loss of autonomy feel like a lifestyle choice.

"Just a girl" isn't a joke; it's a white flag. And personally? I think we should stop waving it.
